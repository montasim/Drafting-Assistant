Architecture decisions need visible assumptions

Maya ChenToday, 10:42Open source post
LinkedIn post summary

The author argues that teams adopt architecture patterns before their constraints are clear, then keep defending those choices after the context changes.

লেখকের মতে, দলগুলো সীমাবদ্ধতা পরিষ্কার হওয়ার আগেই আর্কিটেকচার প্যাটার্ন বেছে নেয় এবং পরিস্থিতি বদলানোর পরও সেই সিদ্ধান্ত ধরে রাখে।

Review before posting: The discussion does not state how long this architecture is expected to last.
Saved replyInsight · edited · 👍 Helpful
A useful way to frame this is that architecture decisions rarely fail because a team chose the “wrong” pattern. They fail when the assumptions behind that choice remain invisible. Writing down the constraint, expected lifetime, and reversal cost makes the decision easier to revisit without turning it into a debate about personal preference.
How do you decide when an architecture choice deserves a formal record rather than a lightweight team note? Expected lifetime and reversal cost seem like useful thresholds, but I’m curious whether your teams use a different signal.
Reversibility is a useful addition to this. A pattern chosen early is less dangerous when the team has documented what would trigger a change and kept the exit cost visible. That turns architecture from a permanent verdict into a decision that can be reviewed as the system learns.
Delaying every decision can hide a different cost: teams invent incompatible local conventions. The balance may be to standardize only the boundaries that are expensive to reverse and leave the rest deliberately open.
